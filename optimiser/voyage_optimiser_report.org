* Introduction

This report documents the architecture and validation of the =voyage_comparison=
propulsion optimiser tool.  The tool performs long-term hindcast-based voyage
simulations comparing the factory combinator pitch/RPM schedule against a
fuel-optimal operating point selection, with optional Flettner rotor
wind-assist.

The tool was originally implemented as a single 4\thinspace913-line Python
script.  A modular refactoring was carried out to improve maintainability,
testability, and extensibility.  This report describes the physical models, the
software architecture after refactoring, and presents validation results from a
full-year simulation on the Rotterdam--Gothenburg route.

** Scope

The tool addresses the following question: /for a given vessel on a fixed route,
how much fuel can be saved by optimising the propeller pitch and RPM at each
operating point, compared to the factory-default combinator schedule?/

The analysis chain covers:
- Calm-water hull resistance from service prediction data.
- Wave added resistance via PdStrip drift transfer functions integrated over
  JONSWAP spectra.
- Blendermann (1994) wind resistance model.
- Flettner rotor wind-assist thrust (Bordogna et al., 2019).
- Hull and propeller roughness penalties (Townsin--Dey, 2003).
- Factory combinator schedule replication.
- Fuel-optimal pitch/RPM selection via propeller model optimisation.

Hindcast weather is provided by NORA3 (Norwegian Reanalysis Archive, 3\thinspace km
resolution) wave and wind fields.

* Physical Models
** Hull Resistance

Calm-water thrust demand is interpolated from the vessel 206 service prediction
at 16 speeds (\SIrange{8}{15.5}{\knot}).  The service prediction includes a
@@latex:15\%@@ sea margin which is removed to obtain calm-water baseline values:

\begin{equation}
  T_\text{calm}(V_s) = \frac{T_\text{service}(V_s)}{1.15}
\end{equation}

Wake fraction $w(V_s)$ and thrust deduction $t(V_s)$ are also interpolated from
service prediction tables.

** Wave Added Resistance

Wave added resistance is computed from PdStrip strip-theory transfer functions.
The =DriftTransferFunction= class loads the =.dat= output from PdStrip and
computes the mean surge drift force for a given sea state:

\begin{equation}
  R_\text{aw} = 2 \int_0^\infty S_\zeta(\omega;\, H_s, T_p)
                  \cdot C_\text{drift}(\omega,\, \theta_\text{rel})
                  \, d\omega
\end{equation}

where $S_\zeta$ is the JONSWAP wave spectrum and $C_\text{drift}$ is the
frequency- and heading-dependent drift force transfer function per unit wave
amplitude squared.

** Wind Resistance

Hull wind resistance follows Blendermann (1994), using tabulated surge force
coefficients $C_X(\alpha)$ for a general cargo vessel:

\begin{equation}
  R_\text{wind} = -C_X(\alpha_\text{app}) \cdot \tfrac{1}{2}\,\rho_\text{air}\,
                   V_\text{app}^2 \cdot A_F
\end{equation}

where $\alpha_\text{app}$ is the apparent wind angle from the bow, $V_\text{app}$
the apparent wind speed, and $A_F = \SI{280}{\metre\squared}$ the frontal
projected wind area.

** Flettner Rotor

The Flettner rotor model is ported from the BruCon C++ vessel simulator.  Lift
and drag coefficients follow Bordogna et al. (2019) with aspect-ratio correction
for a rotor of height \SI{28}{\metre} and diameter \SI{4}{\metre}:

- Lift and drag are tabulated as functions of spin ratio $\text{SR} = \Omega R / V_\text{app}$.
- The target spin ratio is $\text{SR} = 3$ (max \SI{220}{RPM}).
- Rotor drive power follows the Theodorsen @@latex:\&@@ Regier (1944) model.
- Rotor motor fuel is charged at the auxiliary genset SFOC (\SI{215}{\gram\per\kilo\watt\hour}).

The net propulsive benefit is the surge component of the Magnus-effect force
minus the fuel penalty for driving the rotor.

** Hull and Propeller Roughness

Hull roughness resistance follows Townsin @@latex:\&@@ Dey (2003):

\begin{equation}
  \Delta C_F = 0.044 \left[\left(\frac{k_s}{L}\right)^{1/3}
               - 10\,\text{Re}^{-1/3}\right] + 0.000125
\end{equation}

Propeller blade roughness increases torque (and thus fuel) via the empirical
model of Mosaad (1986).  Both roughness values can be specified directly in
$\mu$m or via a =FoulingScenario= which models piecewise-linear roughness growth
over time since last cleaning.

** Factory Combinator

The =FactoryCombinator= class replicates the BruCon vessel simulator's
=MakeNormalCombinator()= algorithm.  For a given thrust demand, it returns the
pitch and RPM dictated by the factory schedule---following the propeller curve up
to a break point, then constant RPM with increasing pitch.

** Propeller Model and Fuel Optimisation

The fuel-optimal operating point is found by searching the full $(P/D,\, n)$
space of the C-series propeller model.  For each thrust demand, the optimiser
finds the pitch/RPM combination that minimises engine SFOC $\times$ brake power,
subject to the MAN L27/38 power envelope.

Results are pre-computed for a grid of 681 thrust values
(\SIrange{10}{350}{\kilo\newton} in \SI{0.5}{\kilo\newton} steps) using parallel
workers, then looked up during the voyage simulation for speed.

The core simulation loop in =evaluate_voyage()= proceeds as follows:

1. Interpolate the route at 1-hour intervals (great-circle).
2. Compute speed-dependent quantities once: wake fraction, thrust deduction,
   calm-water thrust, hull roughness resistance, blade roughness factor.
3. For each hourly position:
   - Fetch NORA3 weather ($H_s$, $T_p$, wave/wind direction and speed).
   - Compute wave added resistance via drift transfer function.
   - Compute Flettner rotor thrust and power.
   - Compute Blendermann wind resistance.
   - Assemble total thrust demand: $T = T_\text{calm} + (R_\text{aw} + R_\text{wind} + R_\text{rough} - F_\text{Flettner}) / (1 - t)$.
   - Evaluate factory combinator and optimiser fuel rates (from cache).
   - Also evaluate no-Flettner variants for savings decomposition.
4. Aggregate fuel totals, weather means, and compute savings split.

Only hours where all four evaluations (factory $\pm$ Flettner, optimiser $\pm$
Flettner) are feasible are counted, ensuring a consistent comparison basis.

** Weather Data

The simulation uses wave and wind data from NORA3, the third-generation
Norwegian Reanalysis Archive produced by MET Norway.  A /hindcast/ is a
numerical weather simulation run retrospectively: the atmospheric and wave
models are forced with observed boundary conditions and assimilate available
measurements, producing a physically consistent, gap-free reconstruction of
historical weather on a regular grid.  Unlike point measurements from buoys or
ships, a hindcast provides complete spatial and temporal coverage, making it
suitable for route-based voyage simulation.

NORA3 uses the WAM spectral wave model driven by the HARMONIE-AROME atmospheric
model.  The wave data used here covers the North Sea and Skagerrak on a rotated-pole
grid with approximately \SI{3}{\kilo\metre} spatial resolution
($\Delta\text{lat} \approx \SI{0.02}{\degree}$,
$\Delta\text{lon} \approx \SI{0.035}{\degree}$, corresponding to
$\approx \SI{2.2}{\kilo\metre} \times \SI{2.2}{\kilo\metre}$ at the route
latitude) and hourly temporal resolution.  The data spans 1993 to the present.

The variables extracted for each route point are:
- $H_s$ -- significant wave height [m]
- $T_p$ -- peak wave period [s]
- $\theta_q$ -- mean wave direction [deg, meteorological convention]
- $U_{10}$ -- 10 m wind speed [m/s]
- $\theta_w$ -- wind direction [deg, meteorological convention]

Monthly files are pre-downloaded for a bounding box around the route
(@@latex:$\pm$@@\thinspace0.5@@latex:$^\circ$@@ margin) and stored as NetCDF files.  During
simulation, the weather at each hourly vessel position is obtained by
nearest-neighbour lookup in both space and time.  Given the \SI{3}{\kilo\metre}
grid spacing, the maximum spatial interpolation error is approximately
\SI{1.5}{\kilo\metre} -- well within the spatial decorrelation scale of the wave
field.  The hourly time step matches the simulation stepping interval exactly, so
temporal interpolation is not required.

** Shaft Generator Mode

The preceding results assume the vessel operates without a shaft generator (SG).
When the SG is online, two additional constraints apply:

1. *Parasitic load:* The SG extracts electrical power from the main engine shaft,
   increasing total engine load.  A typical average SG load of \SI{150}{\kilo\watt}
   is assumed.

2. *RPM floor:* The SG must maintain output frequency within the permitted band
   (\SIrange{48}{60}{\hertz}).  At the PTO gear ratio, this constrains the minimum
   engine RPM to approximately \SI{640}{RPM} (shaft RPM $\geq$ \SI{94.1}{RPM}).

Both constraints apply equally to the factory combinator and the optimiser.  The
factory combinator schedule is constructed with \SI{200}{\kilo\watt} SG headroom
(the margin the hydrodynamicist uses when designing the combinator, on top of the
standard \SI{300}{\kilo\watt} between the propeller curve and the torque limit),
while the actual SG load of \SI{150}{\kilo\watt} is used in the fuel evaluation
for both factory and optimiser.

\begin{verbatim}
python voyage_comparison.py --year 2024 --speed 10 --quiet \
    --sg-load 150 --sg-factory-allowance 200 \
    --sg-freq-min 48 --sg-freq-max 60
\end{verbatim}
#+END_EXPORT

*** Interpretation

The SG RPM floor fundamentally changes the pitch/RPM optimisation landscape.
Without the SG, the factory combinator operates at shaft RPM 70--114, giving the
optimiser room to choose a better (pitch, RPM) combination.  With the SG, both
the factory schedule and the optimiser are constrained to shaft RPM
$\geq$\thinspace\SI{94.1}{RPM}.  At \SI{10}{\knot} transit speed, the calm-water
thrust demand (\SI{113}{\kilo\newton}) places both systems near the RPM floor,
leaving almost no freedom for the optimiser to improve upon the factory schedule.

The pitch/RPM saving effectively vanishes ($-0.3$@@latex:\%@@, within numerical
noise).  The Flettner saving drops from 7.5@@latex:\%@@ to 4.5@@latex:\%@@ in
relative terms, although the absolute Flettner thrust reduction is unchanged ---
the percentage appears smaller because the SG-loaded baseline consumption is
higher (\SI{1348}{t/yr} vs \SI{1061}{t/yr}).

At higher transit speeds the situation improves: the factory combinator must push
above the SG RPM floor, restoring the optimiser's pitch/RPM freedom.  A speed
sweep under SG constraints gives:

*** SG vs Auxiliary Genset Fuel Efficiency

An important question is whether the shaft generator is the most fuel-efficient
way to supply \SI{150}{\kilo\watt} of electrical power.  The alternative is a
separate auxiliary diesel genset.

With the SG on the main engine, the factory combinator burns \SI{1348}{t/yr}.
Without the SG, it burns \SI{1061}{t/yr} --- a difference of \SI{287}{t/yr}.
This incremental fuel for \SI{150}{\kilo\watt} over \SI{7439}{h/yr} corresponds
to an /effective/ SFOC of \SI{257}{\gram/\kilo\watt\hour}.

This is significantly worse than a typical auxiliary genset (\SI{210}--\SI{230}{\gram/\kilo\watt\hour}).
The reason is not the ME's fuel map per se, but the RPM floor constraint: at
\SI{10}{\knot}, the engine would naturally run at lower RPM and higher load
fraction (better SFOC), but the SG forces it to \SI{640}{RPM} minimum, shifting
operation to a lower load fraction with worse SFOC.

The breakeven genset SFOC --- the value at which a separate genset becomes
more expensive than running the SG on the ME --- is \SI{257}{\gram/\kilo\watt\hour}
for the factory combinator.  Since no reasonable genset exceeds this value,
*a separate auxiliary genset is always more fuel-efficient at \SI{10}{\knot}*,
saving approximately \SI{47}{t/yr} (@@latex:\euro@@31k/yr) compared to the SG
at a genset SFOC of \SI{215}{\gram/\kilo\watt\hour}.

At higher speeds the picture changes: the RPM constraint penalty diminishes,
and the ME's superior part-load SFOC may make the SG competitive.

The choice of whether to operate with the SG has a large impact on savings.
When the SG is offline, the Optimiser has full freedom to select the most
fuel-efficient pitch/RPM combination, saving 5.8@@latex:\%@@.  When the SG
is online, the RPM floor removes this degree of freedom.  The Flettner rotor
remains beneficial in both cases, though its relative impact is diluted by the
higher baseline fuel consumption with the SG.

The effective SFOC of the main engine for the SG load is \SI{257}{\gram/\kilo\watt\hour},
well above a typical auxiliary genset (\SI{215}{\gram/\kilo\watt\hour}).  At
\SI{10}{\knot} transit speed, *a separate genset is more fuel-efficient than the
shaft generator*.

* Conclusions

The refactored =voyage_comparison= tool has been validated by running a
full-year (2024) hindcast simulation on the Rotterdam--Gothenburg route.  Key
findings:

1. *Standard mode (SG offline):* Pitch/RPM optimisation saves *5.8@@latex:\%@@*
   (61 t/yr, @@latex:\euro@@40k/yr); Flettner wind-assist adds *7.5@@latex:\%@@*
   (80 t/yr, @@latex:\euro@@52k/yr); combined *13.3@@latex:\%@@*
   (141 t/yr, @@latex:\euro@@92k/yr) at \SI{10}{\knot}.

2. *SG mode (150 kW, 48--60 Hz):* The SG RPM floor eliminates pitch/RPM saving
   ($-0.3$@@latex:\%@@).  Flettner retains *4.5@@latex:\%@@* (61 t/yr).  Combined
   saving *4.2@@latex:\%@@* (57 t/yr, @@latex:\euro@@37k/yr).  The effective SFOC
   of the main engine for the SG load is \SI{257}{\gram/\kilo\watt\hour} ---
   significantly worse than a separate auxiliary genset at
   \SI{215}{\gram/\kilo\watt\hour} --- making the shaft generator a less
   fuel-efficient choice at \SI{10}{\knot}.  At higher speeds
   (\SI{12}--\SI{14}{\knot}), the RPM penalty diminishes and combined savings
   recover to 7.7--9.1@@latex:\%@@.

3. The factory combinator becomes *infeasible* (engine over-power) on 7@@latex:\%@@ of
   voyages, while the optimiser adapts pitch/RPM to remain within the power
   envelope.

4. The *modular architecture* (4 packages, 24 modules, 5\thinspace181 lines)
   provides clean separation of physical models, simulation logic, reporting, and
   visualisation, enabling independent testing and extension of each component.
